Say this, not that, when a debt collector calls

The first call is where people lose money. Carry this instead.

The script

Say only this

"I do not recognize this debt. Send me written validation at my mailing address."

Then ask for the company's name and mailing address, and write down the date and time of the call.

The nevers

Never, on the first call

  • Do not confirm the debt is yours
  • Do not make a "small good faith payment"
  • Do not give bank or card details
  • Do not promise to pay
The rules

Why this matters

On an old debt, one small payment, or even acknowledging it in writing, can restart the lawsuit clock on the entire balance in many states. And a written dispute within 30 days of the validation notice forces collection to stop until they prove the debt. Many debt buyers, who bought the account for far less than it is worth, simply cannot.

The garnishment truth

For ordinary consumer debt, no collector can touch your wages or bank account without suing you and winning in court first. "We garnish Friday" with no judgment is an empty, illegal threat. Write it down, because that call is evidence you can use.

One never: never ignore a court summons. Most collection lawsuits are won by default, because the person never responded, not because the collector proved anything.
